What this brief covers

AI is everywhere in e-commerce.
Results are inconsistent.

The brands winning with AI share one trait: they treated it as a customer experience problem before a technology problem. The ones losing share a different trait: they installed AI tools, turned them on, and assumed the work was done.

Finding 01

AI recommendations are backfiring at scale

Product recommendation engines are the most widely adopted AI tool in e-commerce — and the most consistently misused. When they fail, they don't just miss a sale. They tell the customer you don't understand them.

38%
of shoppers say personalized recommendations feel generic or irrelevant — up from 22% in 2023
Source: Salesforce State of the Connected Customer, 2025

The recommendation paradox: the more AI recommends, the less personal it feels. Brands that plug in Shopify's default AI upsell engine get the same output as every other store on the platform — because the model is trained on the same data pool. Nothing about the recommendation reflects your brand, your curation, or your relationship with that specific customer.

The brands beating this aren't using more AI — they're using it differently. They layer editorial judgment on top of algorithmic output. The algorithm finds what's popular. A human (or a brand-trained model) decides what's on-brand. That combination outperforms pure AI recommendations by 22% in click-through and 18% in conversion.

The design principle

AI should surface options. Your brand should make the final call. Recommendations that feel like they came from a person who knows your store convert. Recommendations that feel algorithmic don't — even when they're technically accurate.

Finding 02

AI-generated product copy is killing conversions

Brands mass-producing product descriptions with AI are seeing higher return rates and lower conversion — not because the copy is wrong, but because it's unmemorable, generic, and indistinguishable from every competitor's.

2.7×
higher product return rate for items with pure AI-generated descriptions vs. brand-edited copy
Source: Shopify merchant data analysis, Q4 2025

The problem isn't the words — it's the expectations. AI-generated product copy tends toward the superlative and the vague: "premium quality," "thoughtfully designed," "perfect for any occasion." None of that tells a customer what they actually need to know. When the product arrives and doesn't match the inflated, generic description, they return it.

Brand-edited copy that uses AI as a drafting tool — not a final author — performs significantly better. The AI handles the structure and SEO scaffolding. The brand voice, the specific sensory details, the honest caveats — those come from a human. That combination reduces return rates and increases the probability that the customer who buys is the right customer for the product.

The design principle

Use AI to draft, humans to finish. A product description written by AI and reviewed by someone who has held the product outperforms one written entirely by either. The human edit is not optional — it's the conversion layer.

Finding 03

Chatbots are adding friction, not removing it

AI chat support is the second most common AI deployment among small e-commerce brands. It's also the feature that generates the most customer complaints — and the most cart abandonment.

$1.8B
in US e-commerce revenue lost annually to cart abandonment triggered by chatbot failure
Source: Forrester Research, 2025

The typical pattern: a customer has a simple pre-purchase question ("Will this fit a queen bed?" or "When does this ship?"). The chatbot either misunderstands the question, loops in circles, or pushes a form. The customer closes the tab. The sale is gone.

Small brands are installing enterprise chatbot platforms designed for companies with thousands of daily queries. At low volume, these tools don't have enough training data to perform well — and they behave erratically, which is worse than no chatbot at all. The brands with the lowest abandonment rates from chat either use a highly customized AI trained on their specific FAQ and policies, or they skip the chatbot entirely and use a simple "Email us" button. Both outperform a poorly configured AI chat.

The design principle

A chatbot that can't answer your 20 most common questions shouldn't be deployed. Audit your support tickets first. Build the chatbot around the questions you actually get, not the questions the vendor's demo answers.
