Executive Summary

Five things every financial services
leader needs to know right now

Chime, SoFi, and Cash App are not beating regional banks on rates or safety. They're winning on experience. AI is how community banks fight back — but only if it's deployed in a way customers can trust.

01 Trust is the product. In financial services, the customer experience IS the product. AI that customers don't understand destroys the one thing banks have left to differentiate on.
02 Explainability is now the law. The EU AI Act classifies credit scoring, loan decisions, and fraud detection as high-risk AI. Explainability isn't optional — it's a legal requirement as of August 2026.
03 Personalization is backfiring. Customers feel surveilled, not served. The line between helpful and invasive is a UX problem, not a data problem — and most institutions are on the wrong side of it.
04 Conversational AI is failing in practice. Bank of America's Erica has 40M+ users. Most customers use it once and revert to human agents. The interface, not the AI, is the bottleneck.
05 Agentic finance is 18 months away. AI agents that move money, negotiate rates, and switch providers autonomously are coming. The institutions designing for this now will own the customer relationship.
→ The common thread: Every one of these is a design problem disguised as a technology problem. The roadblock isn't the AI — it's the experience around it.
Finding 01
The Trust Deficit: Customers Don't Understand What AI Is Doing to Their Money
When an algorithm denies a loan, flags a transaction as fraud, or recommends a financial product — and the customer has no idea why — trust collapses. Not gradually. Immediately. And it rarely comes back.

Financial institutions are deploying AI at scale while simultaneously failing to explain it at the individual level. The result is a growing trust deficit that no marketing budget can repair.
71%
of consumers say they don't understand how AI influences decisions made about their financial accounts
Edelman Financial Services Trust Barometer, 2025
  • When AI denies a credit application with no human-readable explanation, customers don't just lose the loan — they leave the bank.
  • Fraud alerts with no context ("Your transaction has been flagged") generate 3× more customer service calls than fraud alerts with explanation.
  • JPMorgan's internal research found that AI-generated financial recommendations are rejected at higher rates when customers can't see the reasoning — even when the recommendation is correct.
  • Younger customers (25–40) are the most likely to switch institutions over opaque AI — precisely the demographic every bank is trying to acquire.
What this means for your organization
Every AI touchpoint in your customer journey needs an explanation layer. Not a legal disclaimer — a plain-language rationale that a customer can understand in 10 seconds. This is a design problem. Your technology team cannot solve it alone.
Finding 02
Explainability is Now a Legal Requirement — and Most Banks Aren't Ready
The EU AI Act classifies credit scoring, loan origination, and fraud detection as high-risk AI systems. As of August 2, 2026, institutions operating in the EU must provide meaningful explanations for automated decisions — or face fines of up to 3% of global annual turnover.

Only 8 of 27 EU member states have completed implementation. US regulators are watching closely. The CFPB has already signaled similar requirements are coming.
3%
of global annual turnover — maximum EU AI Act fine for non-compliant high-risk AI systems
EU AI Act, Article 99, 2024
  • High-risk AI under the EU AI Act requires: human oversight mechanisms, documentation, transparency to users, and the ability to contest decisions.
  • Most "explainability" implementations today are compliance theater — technical outputs dressed up as customer communication. Regulators are beginning to notice.
  • The CFPB's 2024 guidance on AI in lending explicitly requires that adverse action notices reflect the actual factors used by the AI model — not generic boilerplate.
  • Institutions with mature explainability UX will have a genuine competitive advantage as regulation tightens — they'll move faster and spend less on compliance remediation.
What this means for your organization
Compliance is the floor, not the ceiling. The institutions treating explainability as a customer experience investment — not just a regulatory checkbox — will differentiate on trust while others scramble to meet minimum requirements.
Finding 03
AI Personalization is Making Customers Feel Watched, Not Served
Personalization is the most promised capability in financial services AI — and the most consistently mishandled. The problem isn't the data or the model. It's the moment of delivery.

When a banking app says "We noticed you've been spending more on groceries — here's a budgeting tip" the customer doesn't feel helped. They feel monitored. That line is entirely a design decision, not a data science decision.
54%
of banking customers say AI-powered recommendations feel "intrusive" rather than helpful
PwC Financial Services Consumer Survey, 2025
  • The same insight — "you're spending more than usual" — can feel either helpful or invasive depending entirely on when, where, and how it's surfaced. This is UX, not AI.
  • Customers who feel their data is being used against them are 4× more likely to reduce their product footprint at that institution within 12 months.
  • Opt-in personalization — where customers explicitly choose what the AI monitors — generates 60% higher engagement than passive personalization.
  • Institutions winning at personalization (Marcus by Goldman, Ally) share one trait: they ask customers what they want the AI to notice, rather than assuming.
What this means for your organization
Every personalization use case needs a UX review before deployment — not after customer complaints. The question is not "what can we show the customer?" but "how will the customer feel when we show it?" These are different questions with different answers.
Finding 04
Conversational AI Has 40 Million Users and a Retention Problem
Bank of America's Erica is the most widely adopted AI assistant in financial services — 40 million users, 2 billion interactions. It is also a case study in first-use abandonment.

Most customers interact with Erica once or twice before reverting to the mobile app or calling a human agent. The AI works. The experience of using it — the conversation design, the failure states, the handoff to humans — does not.
40M
Erica users — yet most revert to human agents after initial use. The AI works. The UX doesn't.
Bank of America Q4 2025 Earnings
  • The most common failure in financial chatbots: graceless handoffs. When the AI can't answer, customers are dropped into a queue with no context — forcing them to repeat everything to a human agent.
  • Nielsen Norman Group's 2026 chatbot guidelines found that financial services bots have the highest "rage quit" rate of any sector — customers actively hostile after a failed interaction.
  • Klarna replaced hundreds of customer service agents with AI in 2024 and reversed course within 18 months — not because the AI failed, but because the experience degraded customer satisfaction scores.
  • The institutions seeing real retention from conversational AI share a common design pattern: they design for failure first — the unhappy path — before building the happy path.
What this means for your organization
Before adding capabilities to your conversational AI, audit its failure states. What happens when it doesn't understand? When the customer is frustrated? When they need a human? These moments define the customer relationship more than any feature.
Finding 05
Agentic Finance is Coming — and the UX Doesn't Exist Yet
AI agents that act autonomously on behalf of customers — moving money, switching providers, negotiating rates, filing disputes — are 12 to 18 months from mainstream deployment in financial services.

No institution has figured out the experience design for this. The trust, transparency, and control patterns that make customers comfortable delegating financial authority to AI are an open design problem. The institutions that solve it first will own the agentic finance relationship.
18mo
estimated time to mainstream agentic AI in financial services — the UX design window is now
McKinsey Global Institute, 2025
  • Agentic AI requires customers to delegate authority — to say "you handle this." Financial services customers have never been asked to do this. The onboarding experience for delegation is completely undesigned.
  • Key unsolved UX problems: How does a customer review what an agent did? How do they undo it? How do they set limits? How do they build trust incrementally?
  • The institutions piloting agentic finance today (JPMorgan IndexGPT, Betterment AI advisor) are learning that transparency of action is more important than performance. Customers accept worse outcomes when they understand what happened.
  • Machine Experience (MX) design — designing interfaces for AI agents to navigate, not just humans — is the next required competency in financial services product teams.
What this means for your organization
Start designing the trust and control layer for agentic AI now — before the technology forces your hand. The institutions that have already solved "how do we show customers what our AI did and why?" will have an 18-month head start when agentic systems go mainstream.

Action Plan

Three things to do in the next 90 days

You don't need to solve everything. These three moves create the most leverage with the least organizational friction.

Step 01 — Immediate
Audit your AI explanation layer
Map every customer-facing AI touchpoint. For each one, answer: "What does the customer see when AI makes a decision about them?" If the answer is nothing, a code, or a legal disclaimer — you have a trust problem. Fix the three highest-volume touchpoints first.
Step 02 — 30 Days
Run a personalization perception audit
Talk to 10 customers about your AI personalization features. Ask them to describe what they think the app knows about them. The gap between what they believe and what's true tells you exactly where to redesign. Don't rely on satisfaction scores — ask open questions.
Step 03 — 90 Days
Design your agentic AI trust model
Before you have agentic AI, design the experience of delegating to it. What does setup look like? How does a customer review agent actions? How do they set limits? Getting this architecture right now means you can move fast when the technology is ready.
